5 mistakes you should avoid as a Manager in IT

Dear reader,


Management ( whether technical or non-technical ) is an art of achieving a vision by continuously collaborating and enabling a team / org to collaborate with themselves.

Management's purpose is to achieve a healthy workplace, protect the work-culture and provide opportunities to the rising stars.

Sometimes, due to various circumstances, managers make few mistakes that tend to hurt the outcome of the vision / project and affects the achievement of targets.

If you want to be a successful manager, I suggest you to avoid making the below mistakes no matter what the situation is. These are the reasons why employees tend to leave your team and the company

#1 : Always pointing your direct reports for not completing work on time. " Roses are red, violets are blue.... If they ain't doing it, give them a clue "

If your direct reports have not completed work, there is definitely a reason. Yes, it could be a legit reason as they could be less competent / organized. But there could also be another reason : "insufficient inputs". If they ain't completing, you're responsible too.

Try to find what the root cause is and then make the decision of pointing your direct reports.



#2 : Setting unrealistic expectations. "The Horse lost the race this season...winner Porshe was the reason"

I understand your commitments to leadership matters to you. I also understand that the business is important and you are trying to get work done. But setting up unrealistic expectation from your direct reports will burn them out.

You cannot expect all of them to show the same level of performance. You cannot also make them work day and night by setting very high expectations in your 1 on 1s. This will hurt your direct reports enthusiasm and they will look forward to leaving you as a result.



#3 : Constantly asking "Is it done ?... Is it done ?... Is it done ?" " He lost the fun....when he heard the words, "is it done? "

Don't get me wrong, asking your direct reports whether they have met their daily/weekly commitment is needed. But if you are constantly asking them "is it done ?" every hour or so will lead to frustration.

This is one flavor of micro-management and I personally feel this is a very wrong way to "get things done". Instead, you should get involved in the situation and try to understand "why it was not done". Then think about your direct report's capacity to get it done. Then make your decision whether to balance the workload or to train your employee.

#4 : Measuring all direct reports the same way. " The Birds can fly.... but you shouldn't try "

Few managers have a fantasy. That fantasy is to have a team of all "high performers". I call this a fantasy because I have never seen a team consisting of only "high performers".

Life ain't ticking that way jack. You got high, medium and low performers. You ain't that lucky jack. So if you start comparing between your direct reports, they start thinking that they are measured the wrong way.

Perhaps, all of them ain't getting the same paycheck jack. They come from different experiences. So try to set your boundary of expectations different for each employee.



And perhaps the most important one,

#5 : Not Leading by example. " Mirror mirror on the wall.... Am I setting an example for them all ? "

If you want your direct reports to be like how you want them to be, you need to be the same too. If you are asking them to attend meetings on time, you need to attend too. If you are asking them to work on a weekend due to a deadline, you need to show up as well.

Most importantly, if you are asking them to work hard, make sure that you also do the same.

We all have a lot of expectations in life. Often times we don't see ourselves in the mirror and ask ourselves "Am I meeting their expectations too? ", "Am I setting the bar that I want them to cross? ", "Am I the one they are all looking forward to? "

If the answer is a "yes", then kudos to you and your team. If not, time to grab a mirror and start asking these questions.

I'm sure you've heard this a zillion times, but I'm going the say this anyway :

Employees don't leave the company. They leave their managers. That being said,

Wishing all the Future Managers - Good luck.
